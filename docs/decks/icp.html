ICP / Audience Definition

One workflow.
Three parties. One payer.

A financed invoice always involves three counterparties: the supplier who sells it, the financier who buys it, and the buyer who owes it. Only one of them pays us, and a different one is the distribution channel. Confusing those three roles is why receivables-finance products stall, so we separate them deliberately.

The payer — who writes the cheque
Financiers: specialty finance, private credit, bank SCF desks
Who exactly
Receivables-finance and factoring desks; private-credit and specialty-finance funds; bank supply-chain-finance units; trade and structured-finance teams.
Book size
Deploying $2M to $50M+ into receivables, seeking pre-underwritten, de-risked flow.
What they buy
A lower loss ratio (double pledging is structurally impossible, not audited after the fact) and a lower underwriting cost (the AI agent prices over only the data they are entitled to see).
How we earn
A few bps of financed volume, a slice of the discount, and/or SaaS for the underwriting workstation. We sit in the flow of funds and capture part of the value we create.
Qualifies if
They have been burned by, or actively fear, duplicate assignment; they run manual verification today; their pricing is commercially sensitive.
The primary user — whose pain we solve
Mid-market suppliers with long DSO
Size
$10M to $500M revenue.
Shape
Concentrated, investment-grade buyers and 30 to 90 day payment terms, so the receivable is high quality but the cash is trapped.
Sectors
Manufacturing, logistics, consumer goods, staffing.
Trigger
Growth constrained by working capital; a factoring quote whose price they do not want their buyer to see.
They get
Same-day liquidity at a price the buyer cannot infer. They do not pay us (an origination fee is a secondary line at most).
The anchor — our distribution channel
One large, creditworthy buyer
Why they matter
Their supplier network IS the channel. Buyer-led onboarding turns one relationship into a supplier tail, instead of selling to suppliers one by one.
They get
A healthier supply chain at zero cost. They still pay face value at maturity, to whoever holds the receivable, and never see the pricing.

01The wedge: buyer-led supply-chain finance

We do not try to solve all of factoring. We land one investment-grade buyer, onboard its supplier tail, and bring one or two financiers to compete for that paper. Every side gains at once, which is what makes the first deal closeable:

The buyer gets a healthier supply chain and pays nothing.
Suppliers get cheaper, confidential liquidity without a distress signal.
Financiers get de-risked, pre-underwritten deal flow they cannot double-fund.

Then we expand into a multi-financier marketplace (the supplier shops the receivable, which is our Veild sealed-bid mode), then cross-border, where confidentiality and atomic settlement matter most, then adjacent products such as PO finance and dynamic discounting.

02Beachhead and qualifying signals
Beachhead sectors: manufacturing and logistics. Long DSO, concentrated buyers, high invoice values, and genuine fraud exposure.
High-value invoices, not micro-tickets
Repeat, recurring invoice flow
Counterparties who consider pricing sensitive
Real exposure to duplicate-assignment loss
A regulator or auditor who needs oversight, but has no business seeing the margin
Anti-ICP. Being explicit about who we are NOT for keeps the wedge sharp:
Micro-SMEs with one-off invoices: the unit economics do not work and there is no fraud exposure
Retail or consumer credit
Anyone whose pricing is already public, since confidentiality carries no value
Spot factoring with no repeat flow
Crypto-native yield seekers. Our counterparties are regulated institutions, not anonymous wallets

04What they care about, in their order
Duplicate-financing prevention and legal enforceability of the assignment
Confidentiality of invoice terms and rates
Auditability for regulators and internal risk
Integration into existing ERP and treasury systems

Honest note on sequencing: this is the real-world audience for the product. Canton today is an institutional network, so our first users arrive through a financier or an anchor buyer already on it, not through supplier self-service. The wedge is deliberately built around that.
